AT A GLANCE

As carpool coordinator for my college fellowship, I developed this web application to help facilitate the process for people to sign up for rides to church. The form is also updated with weekly puzzles. I began this project in June 2018, launched it in July 2018, and am still continuously updating it.

Check it out >>

ROLES

Designer and Webmaster: This is a solo project. From needfinding, to user research, to paper prototyping, to design, to front-end development, and to ride arranging, I undertook it all.

DESIGN GOALS

The initial goal was to improve upon the old system, which involved making a Facebook post every week where people would comment for rides. This had its drawbacks in that it was tedious for the carpool coordinator to organize, and it limited church attendance to people who had a Facebook account.

DESIGN PROCESS

After taking on the role of carpool coordinator in May of 2018, I immediately began to think about how I could apply my skills to improve the situation. I had learned the inefficiency of the Facebook comment system from the previous carpool coordinator, and I wanted to make the process easier for both myself and the users.

I first began by asking the previous carpool coordinator what he felt could be improved, and he suggested using a Google form. This is easy for the coordinator, as all of the information is loaded into an accessible spreadsheet, but I recognized that it would be cumbersome for the users, as they would have to receive a new Google form each week and fill it out.

That's when I thought of a web application. Users could easily bookmark the page in their browsers so they could use the same form every week. All previous carpool coordinators had also done something interesting along with arranging rides (weekly jokes, Photoshopped pictures, or funny videos), and I decided that a web application would grant me freedom in sharing with everyone my favorite form of simple entertainment: puzzles.

With this idea in mind, I quickly drew up a paper prototype of how I envisioned the web app to be. I wanted it to be sleek, simple, and easy to use.

After drawing up these simple paper prototypes, I began developing the web application on GitHub. By July 2018, I had a simple, interactive model that was completely functional.

I shared the web application with the fellowship, and started using it to coordinate carpool rides. Things went rather smoothly for the first two weeks, and after people had had enough time to become acquainted with the system, I observed people as they used the web application, and I also interviewed them and asked about aspects of the design in which they saw flaws.

With the feedback, I continued to iterate new designs. The first new feature I implemented after getting feedback was a confirmation email. After signing up for a ride, users would now receive a confirmation email so they could reference if they had already signed up or not. The second new feature was a FAQ page. With newcomers arriving in the fall, it would be helpful for them to have a single page with answers to their potential questions.

SCREENS

\ LANDING \

The landing screen introduces users to the carpool system, which involves a weekly form that they can fill out to indicate whether they need a ride to fellowship that week. After the form, users can attempt a short puzzle created by myself purely for fun.

\ FORM \

The form is constructed specifically to match the system that is currently in place. Users must input basic information such as their name, email address, and which service they need a ride to. There are also other options for going directly home after service or going out for food.

\ SUBMISSION \

On submission, users are notified with a pop-up. The submitted information is then loaded directly into a Google spreadsheet, which is organized so that I can easily group passengers who live in the same location together to facilitate pickups.

\ PUZZLE \

Finally, users have the option of attempting a short puzzle that I create every week. There is no consequence for not completing it, but it simply exists as something fun for people to attempt if they choose to.
