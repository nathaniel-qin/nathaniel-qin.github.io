AT A GLANCE

Word of Hands is a prototype of a mobile add-on to popular reviewing applications like Yelp. It provides the ability to rate and review Deaf accessibility features so that people with these needs can peruse the information more easily.

AFFILIATION \\ DESIGN FOR AMERICA

DFA is a national, interdisciplinary college organization founded upon the principles of human-centered design. This project spanned October 2017 to June 2018.

ROLES

User Researcher: I personally conducted 4 interviews with a Deaf professor, an interpreter, a researcher, and a Deaf student. These were conducted for the sake of better understanding various needs that arise in they have come to learn about in their unique situations.

Designer: With the rest of the team, I collaboratively iterated the design of our screens 3 times with increasing fidelity, continuously validating the problem and our design with our Deaf ally. I also designed the product logo.

My Collaborators: Sanika Moharana, Steven Phung, Ana Dasgupta, Vidya Raghvendra, Miriam Wagner, Josh Duhay, Tommy Sharkey

DESIGN GOALS

Our initial goal was some sort of design solution that would help tackle the issue of accessibility. Through research and interviews, we found that existing platforms for accessibility ratings are outdated, poorly designed, and obsolete. We asked ourselves: How can we promote awareness of and improve accessibility to Deaf-friendly environments?

DESIGN PROCESS

We began the project under the broad topic of general "accessibility." To help us begin narrowing down our scope, we all created our own moodboards to demonstrate to the group what came to our minds when we each thought of accessibility. From there onwards, we discussed which aspect of accessibility that was shared sounded the most interesting and compelling to address. With that, we narrowed down to focus solely on accessibility for the Deaf community.

Going forward, we each conducted multiple interviews with representatives from the Deaf community to better immerse ourselves in the unique aspects of Deaf culture. From these interviews, we gleaned a lot of useful insight and developed several "How Can We..." statements to drive our focus into a specific area.

Once we had figured out what we specifically wanted to target after narrowing down our choice of How Can We statements, we decided to pursue a technological approach due to what we had learned from our interviews- that Deaf people are highly tech-savvy. We researched existing platforms for Deaf people to gain information about Deaf-friendly spaces, and we realized that they were largely outdated and user-unfriendly. For an idea like this to be streamlined, we realized that it had to be incorporated somehow into existing, popular apps. That's how we decided to mockup an add-on mobile application.

Below are a few images of our 2nd iteration of design.

With our simple prototypes completed, we each took the time to prototype a design of 3 or so screens in Sketch, and presented them to the group so we could decide on a design scheme. Then, we took the time to collectively mock up the most important screens that would be able to get across the crux of our idea when we presented to the rest of the teams.

SCREENS

\ SIGN UP \

Users begin by creating a personalized profile in our app, detailing their own accessibility preferences. These criteria were carefully selected after multiple interviews with people involved with the Deaf community.

\ SEARCH \

After syncing with a review app such as Yelp, our icon appears next to search results. The vibrance of the icon represents the level of accommodation the business has in relation to the user's personal preferences.

\ INFO \

This new page contains information related to accessibility. For the user's quick perusal, each accessibility feature has its own crowd-sourced rating. Users can also browse detailed reviews written by other users.

\ REVIEWS \

We opted for a crowd-sourced approach for our reviews, so that users could learn from real experiences that other people before them have had. The goal of this is to be more in-sync with the Deaf community as their needs evolve.

NEXT STEPS

Unfortunately, we ran out of time at the end of the year to continue our project. However, given more time and resources, we planned next to create an interactive prototype of our application and conduct extensive user testing on members of the Deaf community who we had been in contact with. We would use feedback gained from testing sessions to continue to iterate our design, smoothing out usability errors and breakdowns to the best of our abilities.
