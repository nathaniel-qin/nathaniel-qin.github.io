AT A GLANCE \\

Word of Hands is a prototype of a mobile add-on to popular reviewing applications like Yelp. It provides the ability to rate and review Deaf accessibility features so that people with these needs can peruse the information more easily.

AFFILIATION \\ DESIGN FOR AMERICA

DFA is a national, interdisciplinary college organization focused on human-centered design. My team was comprised of students from various backgrounds, united on the common goal of improving accessibility through design.

DESIGN GOALS \\

Our initial goal was some sort of design solution that would help tackle the issue of accessibility. Throughout the year, we conducted research and interviews to solidify our own understanding of the issue. In the end, we found that existing platforms for accessibility ratings are outdated, poorly designed, and obsolete.

SCREENS \\

Users begin by creating a personalized profile in our app, detailing their own accessibility preferences. These criteria were carefully selected after multiple interviews with people involved with the Deaf community.

After syncing with a review app such as Yelp, our icon appears next to search results. The vibrance of the icon represents the level of accommodation the business has in relation to the user's personal preferences.

This new page contains information related to accessibility. For the user's quick perusal, each accessibility feature has its own crowd-sourced rating. Users can also browse detailed reviews written by other users.

We opted for a crowd-sourced approach for our reviews, so that users could learn from real experiences that other people before them have had. The goal of this is to be more in-sync with the Deaf community as their needs evolve.

USER EXPERIENCE \\

This project was heavily a user experience project. Throughout the entire year, we kept in constant contact with various people from the Deaf community to stay in-tune with their needs.

We began this project through surveys and interviews, reaching out to the Deaf community in particular to understand common inconveniences that they might face throughout a typical day.

Once we gained a more solid foundation, we began proposing our ideas to these people, and received feedback about how useful or effective these ideas might be.

Finally, when we began making a mockup of our project, we added a close ally of the Deaf community to our project team. This way, he was able to see everything that was going on behind the scenes and offer us his suggestions as we worked.
